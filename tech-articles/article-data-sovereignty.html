Data Sovereignty Is the New Trade War

India's DPDPA, Saudi Arabia's PDPL, and Brazil's LGPD updates are all creating a global patchwork of data localization rules. The "splinternet" is here — and the compliance burden is crushing.

In 2018, the EU's GDPR set a global template for data protection. Eight years later, that template has fragmented into 142 distinct national and regional regimes, each with its own definitions, consent rules, breach notification windows, and cross-border transfer restrictions. The era of "one privacy policy, applied globally" is over. The new reality is data localization — and it's reshaping how multinationals architect their systems.

India's Digital Personal Data Protection Act, in force since late 2024, requires that personal data of Indian citizens be stored on servers physically located in India. The Saudi Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) added similar requirements in 2025, with fines up to 5% of revenue. Brazil's LGPD amendments in January 2026 expanded the categories of "sensitive data" and shortened breach reporting to 24 hours. Vietnam, Indonesia, Nigeria, Kenya, and Turkey have all passed their own frameworks. Each is subtly different. Each is enforced separately. Each carries its own penalties.

The Compliance Crunch

For multinationals, the new landscape means a single user in São Paulo now triggers 8-12 different compliance workflows. Each new market entry requires legal review, data flow mapping, vendor renegotiation, and infrastructure provisioning. The cost of compliance has become a serious barrier to entry for smaller firms. A recent IBM study found that Fortune 500 companies now spend an average of $4.3 million per year on privacy compliance — and that small-to-mid-sized businesses spend 7% of revenue on the same. This is a regressive tax on digital commerce that benefits incumbents and locks out challengers.

The Data Center Boom

The flip side: a massive build-out of regional data centers. AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Oracle have all committed to in-country data residency for India, Saudi Arabia, Indonesia, and Nigeria. The investments are huge — Microsoft alone is spending $3 billion on Indian data centers in 2026 — but the margins are also strong. Sovereign cloud is now the fastest-growing segment of enterprise IT. A new industry of "compliance-as-a-service" providers has emerged to help smaller firms navigate the patchwork.

"We're no longer building global products. We're building regional products that happen to share a codebase. The compliance layer now dictates the architecture."

The Geopolitical Logic

Data sovereignty isn't really about privacy. It's about leverage. Governments want to ensure that local data — including data held by foreign platforms about their citizens — remains accessible to local law enforcement and intelligence agencies, and is not subject to US Cloud Act orders or Chinese national security demands. India's data localization is a response to anxieties about both Washington and Beijing. Saudi Arabia's PDPL signals a desire to control the data flows of its citizens in an era of mass surveillance. Brazil's updates come amid rising tensions with US platforms. Every country is building a moat. The global internet is balkanizing.

The User's Burden

The end user rarely wins in this regime. The privacy notices get longer, the consent flows get more granular, and the actual protections get weaker as companies struggle to comply with conflicting requirements. A user in France, India, and Brazil (a real demographic for global platforms) may face three different privacy dashboards, three different opt-out mechanisms, and three different data export formats. The promise of GDPR — that users would have meaningful control over their data — has been lost in a sea of legal complexity. The next round of reform needs to start with the user, not the jurisdiction.
