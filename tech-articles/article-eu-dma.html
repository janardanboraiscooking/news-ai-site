The EU's Digital Markets Act: Year One

Europe's gatekeeper rules have reshaped Big Tech. App Store fees dropped, default browsers are now user-selectable, and ad transparency is real. The new Commission wants to go further.

The Digital Markets Act has now been fully enforceable for one year. The early verdict: it's working. Apple's App Store fees in Europe dropped from 30% to 17% (and to 10% for small developers). Google opened up default search selection. Amazon gave third-party sellers visibility into its ranking algorithm. Microsoft unbundled Teams from Office. WhatsApp became interoperable with rival messengers. None of this would have happened without regulatory pressure.

The DMA designates seven "gatekeeper" platforms: Alphabet, Amazon, Apple, ByteDance, Meta, Microsoft, and Samsung's Android (and recently added Booking Holdings and X). These companies are subject to a list of do's and don'ts: no self-preferencing in app stores, no combining personal data across services without consent, no anti-steering provisions, mandatory interoperability for messaging, and active compliance with the regulator's information requests. The penalties for non-compliance are up to 10% of global turnover — and 20% for repeated violations.

The Compliance Theater

Not everyone is happy. American platforms initially responded with what critics called "compliance theater" — technically satisfying the rules while making them as user-hostile as possible. Apple's response to the DMA was to introduce a new "Core Technology Fee" that effectively preserved its pricing power through a different mechanism. The European Commission opened a non-compliance investigation in March 2026, alleging that Apple's fee structure "substantially frustrates" the spirit of the law. The case will be decided this fall.

The Brussels Effect

The DMA is having effects far beyond Europe. The "Brussels Effect" — the tendency of EU regulation to become the global de facto standard because companies prefer a single product — is in full force. Apple now offers its reduced 17% fee globally for developers willing to opt in to alternative app stores. Meta's interoperability extensions are being rolled out worldwide. Companies are choosing to comply with the strictest regime everywhere rather than maintain two product versions. The DMA is becoming the de facto world standard for platform regulation, just as GDPR did for data protection.

"When the EU regulates, the rest of the world tends to follow. The DMA is now the template that Brazil, India, Japan, and the UK are using for their own digital competition laws. Europe is exporting its worldview."

What's Next

The new European Commission, taking office in late 2024, has signaled it wants to go further. Proposals under discussion include: a "DMA 2.0" that adds cloud services and AI foundation models to the gatekeeper list, mandatory data portability for all user-generated content (not just contact lists), and a new "ex-ante" review for AI-powered recommendation systems. Critics call this regulatory overreach. Supporters call it the only serious attempt to constrain Big Tech in the world. The truth, as usual, is in the implementation.

The US Counter-Response

Washington's response has been mixed. The Trump administration has threatened tariffs on countries that "discriminate" against US tech companies — a not-so-subtle shot at the EU. At the same time, individual US states (notably California, New York, and Colorado) are passing their own digital competition laws that borrow from the DMA. Federal legislation remains stalled. The result: a patchwork where American companies face European-style rules in Europe and California-style rules in California, with no unified federal framework. The DMA is filling a global vacuum.
