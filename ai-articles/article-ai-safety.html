Inside the AI Safety Summit

Twenty-eight nations signed the Seoul Declaration on Frontier AI Safety. The press release was triumphant. The actual text is far more modest — and reveals deep fractures about who gets to define "safe."

Last week in Seoul, twenty-eight nations gathered for the second AI Safety Summit. The result: the Seoul Declaration on Frontier AI Safety, a non-binding agreement covering model evaluations, red-teaming standards, and incident reporting. The headlines celebrated a "landmark accord." The reality is more complicated — and the omissions are telling.

The Declaration commits signatories to three things: publish safety evaluations for frontier models before deployment, share information about dangerous capabilities, and fund independent red-teaming research. On paper, this is a meaningful step. In practice, it relies on voluntary disclosure from the very companies developing the technology. There are no enforcement mechanisms, no third-party audits, and no penalties for non-compliance. It is, in the words of one delegate, "a gentleman's agreement among nations that are not gentlemen."

What Made It In

The Declaration's strongest language is around "frontier AI" — defined as models trained using more than 10^26 FLOPs. Signatories agreed to evaluate these systems for capabilities that could enable large-scale harm, including cyber-offensive operations, CBRN (chemical, biological, radiological, nuclear) uplift, and autonomous replication. Companies publishing safety reports before deployment is now the default — at least for the firms that agreed.

Crucially, the Declaration also creates a new international body: the AI Safety Institute Network. This is a real institution with a real budget, dedicated to coordinating evaluations across jurisdictions. It's the first time governments have built standing infrastructure for AI oversight. The UK and US institutes, founded in 2023, will now have peers in Japan, Singapore, and the EU.

What Got Cut

Three things were conspicuously absent from the final text. First: any mention of compute thresholds above which governments would require pre-deployment approval. The "10^26 FLOP" line in the Declaration is a disclosure trigger, not a regulatory trigger. Companies can train models above that threshold without seeking permission. Second: open-weight models were carved out entirely, with signatories agreeing only to "encourage best practices" — a phrase that means nothing. Third: liability. If a frontier model causes harm, the Declaration is silent on who pays. The EU wanted explicit developer liability; the US delegation killed it.

"We spent three days negotiating the difference between 'should' and 'shall' — and we ended up with 'encourages.' This is the G7 of AI safety: all press release, no teeth."

The Missing Signatories

China, Russia, India, and Brazil did not sign. Their absence is more important than anything in the document itself. Frontier AI development is now a multipolar race, and the largest players are not bound by even voluntary commitments. The Declaration has no mechanism to bring them in. Until it does, it regulates the friendly firms while leaving the unfriendly frontier unconstrained. That is not a safety regime. It is a coalition of the willing.

The Path Forward

The Seoul Declaration is not nothing. It establishes precedent, builds infrastructure, and creates diplomatic momentum. But the next summit — scheduled for Paris in early 2027 — must tackle the hard questions: compute thresholds, liability regimes, and how to incorporate open-weight models and non-signatory jurisdictions. Without those, we are regulating the sunset and ignoring the storm. The real work begins when the cameras leave.
