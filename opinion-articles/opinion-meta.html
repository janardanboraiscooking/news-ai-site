Meta's Identity Crisis Demands a Divorce

Instagram and WhatsApp were acquired to eliminate competition. They should be returned. Then Meta can decide what it actually wants to be.

Mark Zuckerberg has a problem that money cannot solve. After spending $19 billion on WhatsApp and $1 billion on Instagram, he has built a company that is two products in search of a strategy and one product (Facebook) that is slowly dying. The company's pivot to the "metaverse" was a disaster. The pivot to AI is more promising but not yet a business. The pivot to "Reality Labs" hardware is losing billions. The result: a sprawling conglomerate with no clear thesis, no organic growth engine, and an FTC retrial scheduled for fall 2026 that may force it to divest the assets it bought to eliminate competition.

The FTC's original complaint, filed in 2020, alleged that Meta's acquisitions of Instagram and WhatsApp were anticompetitive "buy-or-bury" moves — designed to acquire nascent threats before they could mature. The agency lost at trial. The agency won on appeal. The agency is now retrying the case. The evidence has not changed. The acquisitions were made in 2012 and 2014, when Instagram was a 13-employee photo app and WhatsApp was a fast-growing messenger. Both were on trajectories to become the dominant social and messaging platforms of the 2010s. Meta acquired them because the alternative was to compete with them. That is the textbook definition of an exclusionary acquisition, and it deserves a structural remedy.

The Forced Divestiture

If the FTC wins on remand, the question is what remedy makes sense. The agency has signaled it will seek a forced divestiture of Instagram, and possibly WhatsApp. The argument: the only way to restore the competitive dynamic that Meta's acquisitions destroyed is to put the assets back into independent operation. A divested Instagram would compete directly with Facebook for users, creators, and ad dollars. A divested WhatsApp would be free to interoperate with competing messengers (as the DMA already requires) and to develop its own payments, commerce, and business platform. Both companies would thrive as standalone entities. Both would have been larger, more innovative, and more independent if Meta had not acquired them in the first place.

"The argument that Instagram 'would not have survived' on its own is the argument that the acquiring company was uniquely positioned to succeed where the target was not. That is the case for a buy. It is not the case against a divestiture."

Meta's Identity Question

The deeper issue is Meta's identity. Is it a social media company? A messaging company? An AI company? A hardware company? A metaverse company? The current answer is "all of the above, badly." A forced divestiture would force the answer. Meta the social network, with no Instagram to siphon off younger users, would either find a way to remain relevant or finally fade. Meta the AI lab, with Llama models that genuinely lead in open-source benchmarks, could become a serious competitor to OpenAI and Anthropic. Meta the hardware company (Quest, Ray-Ban smart glasses) has a real product line that doesn't depend on social. The company has the talent and the capital to be great at one of these things. The problem is that it is currently mediocre at all of them.

The Competitive Landscape Improves

A divested Instagram would re-enter a social media market that has not had a credible Facebook competitor in a decade. TikTok's regulatory future is uncertain. X has lost most of its cultural relevance. Snap and Pinterest have niches but not scale. An independent Instagram, with no parent to share data with and no cross-promotion to defend, would be free to differentiate, to take risks, and to compete on product. The same is true for WhatsApp, which could become a genuine super-app platform the way WeChat is in China — provided it is not held back by Meta's advertising-first culture.

The Year Of Decision

The retrial will be the defining moment of Lina Khan's FTC legacy and the most consequential tech antitrust case since Microsoft. If the agency wins and the courts impose a structural remedy, we will look back on 2026 as the year the social internet was re-set. If the agency loses, the buy-or-bury playbook will be open to every incumbent, and the next decade of consumer tech will look like the last. The stakes are higher than any individual company's fate. They are about whether antitrust law still has the tools to discipline the most powerful corporations in the world. We will know by Christmas.
