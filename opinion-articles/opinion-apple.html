Nº 43 Opinion · Apple
Opinion May 17, 2026 ◆ 5 min ◆ By Editorial Board

Apple's Walled Garden Is Costing You Your Rights

The company that built a brand on "it just works" is now the world's largest toll collector on creativity. The DMA fixes that. Apple's response to the DMA proves how right it is.

Apple's argument against the Digital Markets Act has always been that "users choose Apple because we make better products, not because we lock them in." It is a beautifully crafted narrative, and it is also false. The iPhone's market dominance is not the result of consumer choice. It is the result of a vertically integrated monopoly on the only mobile computing platform that matters. Apple owns the silicon (A-series and M-series chips), the operating system (iOS and iPadOS), the browser engine (WebKit), the app store (the only one), the payment system (the only one), and the default distribution channel for the most lucrative consumer market in the world. That is not a garden. That is a fortress.

The toll Apple collects is enormous. The 30% App Store fee (now 17% in the EU under DMA pressure, with a 3% additional "Core Technology Fee" layered on) is the highest in the software industry. The IAP commission on digital goods is 15-30%, levied on transactions Apple does not facilitate. The "Apple Tax" has been estimated to transfer roughly $30 billion per year from developers to Apple's services revenue — money that would otherwise fund R&D, lower prices, or compensate creators. None of this is justified by cost. The App Store is one of the highest-margin products in corporate history. Apple's response to the DMA — to introduce a new fee structure that effectively maintains its pricing power while technically complying with the law — is the clearest possible admission that the company knows its position depends on rent extraction, not merit.

The Sideloading Fight

The deepest harm is the prohibition on sideloading. In Europe, DMA-driven changes now allow users to install apps from outside the App Store — but Apple has made the experience as hostile as possible. New "notarization" requirements, scare screens, fee structures that punish developers for using alternative distribution, and a confusing "default browser" choice screen that resets every time Safari is opened. In the US, sideloading is still impossible. A user who wants to install an app that Apple has rejected — for arbitrary, opaque, or politically motivated reasons — has no recourse. The platform owner is the gatekeeper of the platform, and the gatekeeper's interests are not aligned with the developer's or the user's.

"The argument that sideloading is a security risk is the argument that the only safe way to use a phone is to let Apple decide what you can run on it. The same argument was made about personal computers in the 1990s. It was wrong then, and it is wrong now."

The DMA Working Group

The DMA's Article 6 and 7 obligations are working. App Store fees in Europe are down. Default browser and search choices are real. Messaging interoperability with third-party messengers is rolling out. None of this has destroyed the iPhone business, contrary to Apple's predictions. iPhone sales in Europe are up year over year. Customer satisfaction scores are unchanged. The changes have not made the platform less secure — iOS security incident rates have remained at historical lows. The fearmongering was always cover for the rent collection.

The DOJ Case Matters More

The more consequential action is the DOJ's 2025 antitrust case against Apple, which targets the smartphone market as a whole — not just the app store. The complaint alleges that Apple has used its control of the iPhone to suppress cross-platform technologies (super-apps, cloud streaming, third-party smartwatches, alternative payment systems) in order to keep users locked into its services. The case is set for trial in early 2027, and the remedy phase could be the most dramatic in tech antitrust history. A forced opening of iMessage, mandatory sideloading on a global basis, or a separation of the App Store from the device itself are all on the table.

What The DMA Should Do Next

The European Commission is currently investigating Apple's "Core Technology Fee" and its DMA compliance more broadly. The investigation should result in a finding of non-compliance and the maximum fine (10% of global turnover, roughly $39 billion). The Commission should also use the DMA's 2027 review to add new obligations: mandatory app store neutrality, a right to install apps from any source without platform-imposed friction, and a true separation of platform functions from app store functions. The DMA is the most important tech regulation in the world right now. The test of whether it works is whether companies like Apple comply with it in spirit, not just in letter. The early signs are not encouraging. The next two years will tell.
