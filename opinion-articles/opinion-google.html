Nº 41 Opinion · Google
Opinion May 19, 2026 ◆ 5 min ◆ By Editorial Board

The Google Breakup Is the Only Honest Outcome

Chrome should be sold. Android should be free. Search should be opened. The only way to save the open web is to take it back from the company that quietly consumed it.

Judge Amit Mehta's liability finding was the easy part. The hard part is the remedy, and the political gravity is pulling toward a settlement — some behavioral commitments, a fine, a public statement, an undertaking to "do better." This is what Google wants. This is what the company has spent two years and hundreds of millions of dollars in lobbying to achieve. It would be a catastrophe.

The reason is structural. Google did not achieve 90% of the US search market by building a better product. It achieved it by paying Apple, Samsung, Mozilla, and every other browser-maker on the planet to make Google the default — to the tune of $26 billion per year. That is not competition. That is the systematic purchase of monopoly rent. The product that emerges from that arrangement is the product that wins by default, not by merit. Behavioral remedies — non-defaulting requirements, choice screens, contractual limits on Apple — would slow the bleeding. They would not stop it. The only remedy that addresses the actual harm is structural: the divestiture of Chrome, and the unbundling of Android from search.

What A Real Breakup Looks Like

A forced divestiture of Chrome would create the first credible competitor to Google Search in two decades. Chrome ships on billions of devices and controls the default search flow for the entire consumer internet. Sold to a third party — Apple, Microsoft, or a consortium — it would become a search-neutral platform that any search engine could pay to be default on equal terms. The browser would still exist. The user would still choose a search engine. But the choice would be real, not the theater of a "choice screen" buried three clicks deep in a settings menu.

The unbundling of Android from search is the second piece. Google Play Services and the Android ecosystem currently function as a vehicle for Google's other products, including search. A neutral Android — distributed as open-source AOSP, without Google apps pre-installed — would let device makers and users choose their default search engine based on the same competition that supposedly exists today. It would also let competitors like Bing, DuckDuckGo, and the new AI-native search engines (Perplexity, Brave Search, You.com) have a real shot at distribution.

"The market for search is not broken because Google is better at search. The market is broken because Google paid to be the only search engine anyone ever sees. We are not consumers of search. We are products, sold to advertisers, and the search monopoly is the distribution channel."

The Ad Tech Case Is More Urgent

The Google Search case dominates the headlines, but the parallel ad-tech case is where the immediate harm lives. Google controls the publisher ad server (DoubleClick for Publishers), the ad exchange (AdX), and the dominant demand-side platform (Google Ads). The result is a self-preferencing ad stack that extracts roughly 30% of every publisher's digital ad revenue in technology fees, much of it flowing to Google. The court has already found this conduct monopolistic. The remedy should be structural: a forced divestiture of AdX, restrictions on Google's ability to operate both sides of the auction, and a real separation between publisher ad tools and advertiser ad tools.

The Counter-Arguments Are Wrong

Google will argue that breaking up Chrome would harm users, that Android is a free product, that competitors can already enter the market, and that the company has invested billions in AI and infrastructure that should not be at risk. None of this holds. A divested Chrome would still be a free, high-quality browser. A neutral Android would still be a leading mobile OS. Competitors cannot enter the market because the market is structured to prevent their entry. And Google's AI investments are funded by monopoly rents from search and ads — they are not independent of the harm. The whole point of structural remedies is that the assets were acquired through exclusionary conduct and the rents they generate are themselves the problem.

What We Lose If We Settle

A settlement would entrench the current arrangement. Behavioral remedies have a 30-year track record of failure. The 2001 Microsoft consent decree did not open the OS market. The EU's 2004, 2009, and 2018 Google antitrust decisions did not reduce Google's search share below 90%. The DMA's interoperability requirements are being implemented as a minimum-viable-compliance exercise. The lesson of four decades of tech antitrust is clear: behavioral remedies don't work. Only structural remedies do. The next eighteen months will determine whether we learned that lesson, or whether we will be writing this column again in 2030.
